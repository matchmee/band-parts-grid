🎵 Worship Band Parts Grid

Listen. Map the parts. Think like a Music Director.

How to use this tool as MD training

  1. Listen once through without writing. Just feel the arc — where is the song smallest? Where is it biggest?
  2. Second listen: map one instrument at a time. Follow just the drums through the whole song, then just the bass, and so on. Fill in that row as you go.
  3. Third listen: check transitions. How does each section hand off to the next — a fill, a drop, a build, a hard stop?
  4. Zoom out. Read your grid top to bottom per column. Does each section have a clear size? If everything is "Full," nothing is.

The MD questions to ask in every section

  • Who is carrying this section (the main rhythmic or melodic engine)?
  • Who is coloring it (pads, swells, textures)?
  • Who is out — and why does the silence help?
  • What changes between Chorus 1 and Chorus 2? Something should.
  • Where is the dynamic peak of the song, and how do the parts create it?
🥁 Drums — what to listen for

Is it full kit, hats only, cross-stick, or toms? Half-time vs. straight time? Where do the big fills land, and does the kick pattern change between verse and chorus? Drums define the size of every section more than any other instrument.

🎸 Bass — what to listen for

Whole notes vs. moving groove. Does it sit on the root or walk? Does bass drop out in a stripped section and re-enter for impact? Bass entering with the kick is the classic "now we're big" moment.

🪕 Acoustic — what to listen for

Strumming pattern size: light picking, half strums, or full drive? Acoustic often is the glue in verses and gets buried (or should tuck in) during big choruses.

🎛️ Electric — what to listen for

Ambient swells and delay textures vs. defined riffs and lead lines. Is there a signature hook that must be covered? Power chords in the final chorus? Electric is usually the color and the peak energy.

🎹 Keys / Synth — what to listen for

Pads vs. piano vs. arps vs. synth bass. Keys often hold the transitions together — listen for what fills the space when other instruments drop out.

🎤 BGVs — what to listen for

When do harmonies enter? Usually not in Verse 1. Unison vs. stacked harmony, echoes/answers, oohs in the bridge. BGVs entering is a dynamic step up — spend it wisely.

🪘 Aux / Percussion — what to listen for

Shaker or tambourine adding motion, cymbal swells into sections, loops or claps. Aux usually adds energy without adding volume.

Rule of thumb

A great arrangement is a story of space: it earns the big moments by getting small first. If your grid shows everyone playing everything all the time, listen again.
